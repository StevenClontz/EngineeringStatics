Subsection 3.4.1 Coordinates in Three Dimensions

Subsubsection Cartesian Coordinates

Extending two dimensional Cartesian coordinates, you can express any point or vector in 3D space with components in the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) orthogonal directions. The notation remains the same as for 2D vectors, with the addition of a \(z\)-component vector and the corresponding unit vector \(\khat\text{.}\)

Subsubsection Direction Cosine Angles

The direction of a vector in 2D systems could be expressed clearly with a single angle measured from a reference axis, but the added spatial complexity of a 3D system means that a single angle is not enough to specify the direction of a vector.

One common way to define the direction of a 3D vector is by using direction cosine angles, also commonly known as coordinate direction angles. The direction cosine angles are the three angle between the positive \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) axes to a given vector and are called \(\theta_x\text{,}\) \(\theta_y\text{,}\) and \(\theta_z\text{,}\) respectively. Three dimensional vectors, components, and angle are difficult to visualize. The interactive shown in figure should help you develop your visualization skills, which will be helpful to have when solving problems.

Given the definition of direction cosine angles we can also conclude that:

  1. Direction cosine angles must always be between \(0\degree\) and \(180\degree\)

    \begin{equation*} 0\degree \le \theta_n \le 180\degree. \end{equation*}

  2. Any direction cosine angle greater than \(90\degree\) indicates a negative component along that respective axis. Spatially this is because all direction cosine angles are measured from the positive side of each axis. Mathematically this is because the cosine of any angle less than \(90\degree\) is numerically negative.

We can also directly relate the components of a vector to its direction cosine angles using the following equations.

\begin{gather} \cos {\theta_x} = \frac{A_x}{\left |A \right |}\tag{3.10}\\ \cos \theta_y = \frac {A_y}{\left |A \right |}\tag{3.11}\\ \cos \theta_z = \frac {A_z}{\left |A \right |}\tag{3.12} \end{gather}

Note the component in the numerator of each direction cosine equations is positive or negative as defined by the coordinate system, and the full-vector magnitude in the denominator is always positive. Looking closely at the right side of these three equations, notice that each equation consists of the component divided by the total vector magnitude, which you may recall are the same equations used for unit vectors. Thus, the cosine of each direction cosine angle collectively also computes the components of the unit vector; hence we can write an equation for \(\hat{\vec{A}}\text{,}\) i.e., the unit vector along \(\vec{A}\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \hat{\vec{A}}=\cos \theta_x \ihat +\cos \theta_y \jhat + \cos \theta_z \khat \end{equation*}

Combining the Pythagorean Theorem with our knowledge of unit vectors, and direction cosine angles gives this result: if you know two of the three direction cosine angles you can manipulate the following equation to find the third.

\begin{equation} \cos^2 \theta_x + \cos^2 \theta_y +\cos^2 \theta_z = 1\tag{3.13} \end{equation}

Subsubsection Spherical Coordinates

Spherical coordinates can also be used to define the direction of a 3D vector. Spherical coordinates include a set of three terms (see figures NN, OO, and PP below),

  1. an angle \(\theta\) measures the angle from the +\(x\) axis to the plane the vector shares with the \(z\)-axis,
  2. an angle \(\phi\) measures how far from the positive \(z\)-axis the vector lies, and
  3. a distance along the line defined by \(\theta\) and \(\phi\text{.}\)
Warning 3.20.

This diagram is wrong. Angle \(\theta\) should be shown conforming with the right hand rule.

Figure NN – While the cylindrical coordinate system is not used in Statics, it is valuable to recognize the difference between the cylindrical and spherical (or polar) coordinate systems is the designation of the distance off of the \(r-\theta\) plane in cylindrical uses a linear distance z, where polar uses the angle \(\phi\) off the z-axis

When the two given spherical angles are defined the manner shown in Figure 3.22, the components of the vector \(\vec{A}\) are:

\begin{align} A' \amp= A \sin \phi \tag{3.14}\\ A_z \amp= A \cos \phi\tag{3.15}\\ A_x \amp= A'\cos \theta = A \sin \phi \cos \theta\tag{3.16}\\ A_y \amp= A'\sin \theta = A \sin \phi \sin \theta\tag{3.17} \end{align}

Reflect on the equations above. Can you think through the process of how they were derived? The generalized steps are as follows.

  1. Draw an accurate sketch of the given information in either orthographic or isometric view and define the right triangles related to both \(\theta\) and \(\phi\text{.}\)

  2. Use trig identities on the right triangle involving the vector, the \(z\) axis and angle \(\phi\) to find

    1. the component of \(\vec{A}\) adjacent to \(\phi\text{,}\) and

    2. the projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto the \(xy\) plane.

  3. Use trig identities on the right triangle involving vector \(\vec{A}'\) and \(\theta\) to find the remaining components of \(\vec{A}\text{.}\)

Unfortunately, not all problems give the exact angles \(\theta\) and \(\phi\) as described above; thus, it is useful to know how to derive equations for the given angles in any problem.
