As the three components of \(F\) are perpendicular, we can apply the Pythagorean Theorem to find the magnitude of \(F\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} F \amp = |\vec{F}| = \sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2+F_z^2}\\ \amp = \lbf{\sqrt{20^2 + \left( -30 \right) ^2 + 60^2}}\\ \amp = \lbf{70} \end{align*}
The next component, the angle \(\theta\text{,}\) measures the angle between the \(x\) axis and the projection of \(\vec{F}\) onto the \(xy\) plane. Using a right triangle of the projection of \(/vec{F}\) into the \(xy\) plane, we find \(\theta\) using the inverse tangent \(\tan^{-1}\) of the side opposite \(\theta\text{,}\) \(F_y\text{,}\) divided by adjacent side, \(F_x\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \theta = tan^{-1} \frac{F_y}{F_x} = tan^{-1}\left( \frac{-30}{20}\right) = \ang{-56.31} \end{equation*}
Finally, the last component, the angle \(\phi\text{,}\) is measured down from the \(+z\) axis to the vector \(\vec{F}\text{.}\) Using a right triangle between \(\vec{F}\) and the \(z\) axis, we find \(\phi\) using the inverse cosine \(\cos^{-1}\) of the adjacent side, \(F_z\text{,}\) divided by the hypotenuse \(|\vec{F}|\text{.}\) Recognize that \(\phi\) is the same angle as the direction cosine angle \(\theta_z\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \phi = \theta_z =cos^{-1} \frac{F_z}{|\vec{F}|} = cos^{-1}\left( \frac{60}{70} \right) = \ang{31.0} \end{equation*}
In summary, the three spherical components of \(\vec{F}\) are the magnitude of \(\vec{F}\) and the angles \(\theta\) and \(\phi\text{.}\) Notice that the sperical angle \(\theta\) is smaller than the direction cosine angle \(\theta_x\text{,}\) \(\ang{56.31}\lt \ang{73.4}\) as \(\theta\) is only in the \(xy\) plane, but \(\theta_x\) is a 3D angle from the \(x\) axis up to the vector \(\vec{F}\text{.}\)
