Solution 2.4.10.1.

You’ll notice that the spherical and cylindrical coordinates only vary by one component. The spherical system measures height with an angle, whereas the cylindrical system measures height in the units of the vector, in this case, pounds-force \(lbf\text{.}\)
Again, the three components of \(F\) are perpendicular, we can apply the Pythagorean Theorem to find the magnitude of \(F\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} F \amp = |\vec{F}| = \sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2+F_z^2}\\ \amp = \lbf{\sqrt{20^2 + \left( -30 \right) ^2 + 60^2}}\\ \amp = \lbf{70} \end{align*}
The angle \(\theta\text{,}\) is the same as \(\theta\) in spherical and measures the angle between the \(x\) axis and the projection of \(\vec{F}\) onto the \(xy\) plane. Using a right triangle of the projection of \(/vec{F}\) into the \(xy\) plane, we find \(\theta\) using the inverse tangent \(\tan^{-1}\) of the side opposite \(\theta\text{,}\) \(F_y\text{,}\) divided by adjacent side, \(F_x\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \theta = tan^{-1} \frac{F_y}{F_x} = tan^{-1}\left( \frac{-30}{20}\right) = \ang{-56.31} \end{equation*}
Finally, the height \(z\) is measured from the \(xy\) plane to the tip of vector \(\vec{F}\) and thus is the vertical force component \(F_z\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} z = F_z = \lbf{60.0} \end{equation*}
In summary, the three cylindrical components of \(\vec{F}\) are the magnitude of \(\vec{F}\text{,}\) the angle \(\theta\text{,}\) and the height \(z\text{.}\)
