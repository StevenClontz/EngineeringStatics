Section 4.2 Scalar Moments

Key Questions
  • Why is there no moment about any point on the line of action of a force?
  • If you increase the distance between a force and a point of interest, does the moment of the force go up or down?
  • What practical applications can you think of that could use moments to describe?

Moments in two dimensions can only cause a clockwise or counter-clockwise rotation which means that the moment vector is always perpendicular to the page. Therefore, we can completely describe this two-dimensional moment with a signed scalar value. This signed value is called the scalar moment.

The numerical value of the scalar moment indicates its magnitude and the sign indicates its direction. By convention, a positive sign indicates a counter-clockwise moment and a negative sign means clockwise. This sign convention is chosen to be consistent with the right hand rule.

As you probably know, the strength of the moment (torque) produced by a wrench depends on where and how much force you apply to the wrench. If the nut won't budge, you can apply a larger force or get a longer wrench, and the optimum direction to apply the force is at at right angles to the wrench handle.

The magnitude of a scalar moment is found by by multiplying the magnitude of force \(\vec{F}\) times the moment arm \(d_{\perp}\) as illustrated in FigureÂ 4.2.1. The moment arm is defined as the perpendicular distance from the center of rotation to the line of action of the force, measured perpendicularly.

\begin{equation} M_A ={F} d_{\perp}\text{.}\tag{4.2.1} \end{equation}

To this value you must attach a positive or negative sign to indicate the rotational direction.

Notice that since the magnitude of a moment depends only on the magnitude and moment arm, every point on the wrench experiences a different moment. Points on the forceâ€™s line of action experience no moment because the moment arm is zero.

Subsection 4.2.1 Varignonâ€™s Theorem

Varignonâ€™s Theorem is a method of calculating moments developed in 1687 by French mathematician Pierre Varignon (1654 â€“ 1722). It states that the sum of the moments of several concurrent forces is equal to the moment of the resultant of those forces. In other words, to find the moment of a series of concurrent forces about a given point, we can add the forces to find the resultant and then calculate the moment of that resultant about the point of interest, or compute the moment of each individual force about the point of interest and then sum those moments to find the net moment about that point. Either method will give us equivalent results.

This can be a convenient tool if you know the \(x\) and \(y\) components of a force and want to calculate the moment of that force about a point. Varignonâ€™s Theorem tells you that you can calculate the moment of \(F_x\) and \(F_y\) about that point individually, and then add the results to find the net moment about the point. As an added bonus, the \(x\) and \(y\) components of the position vector and the force vector are naturally perpendicular, so you do not need to apply the full cross product. Simply multiply the perpendicular components and assign signs for each moment from the right-hand rule. In the case of a two-dimensional moment this would look like

\begin{align*} \vec{M}\amp = \vec{r}\times\vec{F}\\ \vec{M}\amp = \vec{r}_x\times\vec{F}_y + \vec{r}_y\times\vec{F}_x \end{align*}
