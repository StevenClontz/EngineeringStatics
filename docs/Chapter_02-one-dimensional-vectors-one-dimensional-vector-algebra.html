Subsection 3.2.1 One dimensional Vector Algebra

Vector Addition.

.

Adding multiple vectors together finds the resultant vector, resultant vectors can be thought of as the 'sum of' or 'combination' of other vectors. To find the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) of two 1D vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) you can simply use the tip-to-tail technique shown in Figure 3.2 below. In the tip-to-tail technique, you slide vector \(\vec{B}\) until its tail is at the tip of \(\vec{A}\text{,}\) and the vector from the tail of \(\vec{A}\) to the tip of \(\vec{B}\) is the resultant \(\vec{R}\text{.}\) Note that the resultant \(\vec{R}\) is the same when you add \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) - in this way we can say that the order of vector addition does not matter and is considered commutative.

Vector Multiplication by a Scalar.

Multiplying or dividing a vector by a scalar simply changes the magnitude of the original vector but maintains the original line of action. One of the common transformations we make of vectors is to find the negative of a vector. To find the negative of vector \(\vec{A}\text{,}\) we would simply multiply \(\vec{A}\) by -1, in equation form this looks like:

\begin{equation*} -\vec{A} =(-1) \vec{A} \end{equation*}

Spatially, when you create a negative vector, you are simply flipping the tip and tail ends. The magnitude, line of action, and orientation stay the same, but the sense flips so now the arrowhead points in the opposite direction. This could be simulated in Figure 3.2 by changing one of the vectors from a positive to a negative value.

Vector Subtraction.

The easiest way to handle vector subtraction is to break it into a two-step process where you first take the negative of the vector you are subtracting then add it to the other vector. In this way, you can still use the tip-to-tail technique after flipping the vector you are subtracting. You can also simulate this in Figure 3.2 by following the steps below.

  1. Find \(\vec{A}-\vec{B}\) where \(\vec{A}=2~\text{ cm} \) and \(\vec{B}=2~\text{ cm} \text{.}\)
  2. Set \(\vec{A}\) to a value of \(2~\text{ cm} \) and \(\vec{B}\) to a value of \(-3~\text{ cm} \) (the negative of its actual value).
  3. Slide the tail of either vector to find the resultant (or sum) of these two vectors. The order does not matter as vector addition is commutative.
  4. The result should be \(-1~\text{ cm} \text{.}\)
