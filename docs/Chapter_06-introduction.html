Section 5.1 Introduction

This chapter will build on what you learned in the previous rigid body equilibrium chapters Chapter 3 and [cross-reference to target(s) "Chapter_05" missing], by helping you to solve multiple-body structures. Broadly defined, a structure is any set of interconnected rigid bodies. The different bodies composing the structure may be able to move relative to one another, such as the blades in a pair of scissors shown below or they may be fixed relative to one another, such as the structural members within a bridge. Static analysis of structures involves the application of the equilibrium equations to solve for the forces acting on and between individual members of the structure.

Equilibrium Types.

You might be wondering how the equilibrium of structures is different than the equilibrium of particles Chapter 3 and rigid bodies [cross-reference to target(s) "Chapter_05" missing]. We will investigate the similarities and differences of equilibrium analysis for several types of systems. A summary table of these differences in Table 6-1.

As the approach and equations vary between systems, it is important to first determine the type of equilibrium problem to be solved. While the details related to the equilibrium of a particle or single rigid body should be reviewed; do not worry if the techniques and equations for trusses, frames, and machines are not clear at this point. We will frequently reference table 6-1 throughout this chapter and will share it again at the end of the chapter. A key skill you will learn in this chapter is to properly classifying a structure as a truss, which are made of all two-force members, versus a frame or machine, which each have at least one multi-force body. In trusses, frames and machines, properly identifying and analyzing two-force members is also very important.

Two-force Members.

Every member of a truss is a two-force member. Recall from [xref 4.2.4 Two-force bodies] that a two-force member is a body which is subjected only to a pair of compression or tension forces with and no couples exerted anywhere on the body.

For a two-force member to be in equilibrium, the force vectors need to be 1) equal in magnitude, 2) opposite in direction, and 3) collinear or sharing the same line of action. By this definition, two-force members are not required to be slender, straight members, but can always be recognized by having only two pins connected to other bodies/supports and having no other loading beyond external forces applied at the pins.

The common way to express the unknown axial force in each two-force member is with a magnitude and a sense, where the sense is expressed as either tension (T) or compression (C). To further investigate the nature of the pure axial forces of tension (T) and compression (C) let us look at the effect of tension pulling and compression pushing on each joint and member in the figure below.

When analyzing trusses, we are looking to identify the axial forces acting within each member for a given loading. In mechanics classes taken after Statics, you will be able to use these axial forces to select a material and design the cross sectional properties of each member. Later in Ch 8: Internal Loading within Rigid Bodies chapter of this book, you will analyze additional internal loadings in members, including shear force and bending moment.

Thinking Deeper: Equilibrium of Multi-Body Systems.

When a complete truss is in equilibrium, each member of the truss, each joint, and each section are also in equilibrium. This continues all the way down to a single atom of the structure. This universal equilibrium across spatial scales is one of the governing principles which allows us to break multi-body systems into smaller solvable parts.

You will see in this chapter that we have the freedom to isolate free-body diagrams at any scale to expose our target unknowns.

Interactions between members.

As we are now dealing with multi-body systems, we need to revisit Newton's 3rd Law (from Chapter 1), “For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.” We apply this law to multi-body systems as the reactions applied to one body will be transferred equal and opposite to the interacting bodies. So practically we could restate Newton's 3rd law for interaction forces, “For every force on a body, there is an equal, opposite, and collinear force acting on the connecting body.”

Load Paths.

A basic concept that can help you think about structural systems is load paths. Load paths are conceptually how an applied load (like the gravity load in the image below) is passed through the interconnected members of a structural system and end up being supported by the reactions (which in the image below are the earth beneath the footings). All structural systems, whether non-moving frames or moving machines have some sort of load path. This is also a good concept to think about for your problem solving, where we need to computationally move from our known values (often applied loads) down through the interconnected bodies of the system, solving for unknowns as you go.
