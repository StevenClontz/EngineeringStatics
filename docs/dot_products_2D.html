Subsection 3.3.4 Dot Products

The sections above cover 2D vector addition and subtraction in detail, but vector multiplication requires some additional mathematics. It turns out that there are two different ways to calculate the product of two vectors; one method produces a scalar produce, while the other method results in a vector.

  • Dot products are used to compute how much of one vector is parallel to another vector. This topic will be covered in this section and again in 3D Dot Products.
  • Cross products are used to compute how much of one vector is perpendicular to another vector. This topic will be deferred until the chapter Moments and Couples.

Dot products project one vector onto another and result in a scalar value. They can use them to find the projection of one vector onto another or the angle between two vectors. Let us go back to our example of the two cables in the interactive in Figure 3.9. If you would like to compute the portion of of tension in cable \(A\) which is pulling in the same direction as cable \(B\text{,}\) known as the projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) you could start with the dot product.

One equation for the dot product is

\begin{equation} \vec{A}\cdot \vec{B}= | \vec{A} | | {B} |\cos \theta\tag{3.2} \end{equation}

In this equation, the bold symbols represent vector quantities, while the corresponding non-bold symbols represent their magnitudes which are scalars. The angle \(\theta\) in the equation is the angle between between these two vectors. For the angle \(\theta\) between these vectors, you can use either representation shown in Figure 3.12.

Before diving into how we will use dot products in Statics, we should emphasize that mathematically dot products are:

  1. Commutative. The order you dot vectors does not matter

    \begin{equation} \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} = \vec{B}\cdot\vec{A}\tag{3.3} \end{equation}
  2. Associative. It does not matter whether you multiply a scalar value a by the final dot product, or either of the individual vectors, you will still get the same answer.
    \begin{equation} a\left( \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B} \right ) = a\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}= \vec{A}\cdot a\vec{B}\tag{3.4} \end{equation}
  3. Distributive. If you are dotting one vector \(\vec{A}\) with the sum of two more \((\vec{B}+\vec{C})\text{,}\) you can either add \(\vec{B}+\vec{C}\) first, or dot \(\vec{A}\) by both and add the final value.
    \begin{equation} \vec{A}\cdot\left( \vec{B} + \vec{C} \right ) = \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}+ \vec{A}\cdot \vec{C}\tag{3.5} \end{equation}

Subsubsection Vector Magnitude vs. Scalar Value

Let us spend a few minutes talking about a couple more definitions and notation which are important when dealing with dot products. In Table 3.13 below we will contrast the magnitude of a vector with the scalar result of a dot product.

The magnitude of a vector and the scalar result of a dot product may seem like similar terms as they are both are numeric values which come from a vector or vectors. The key difference is that vector magnitudes are always positive or zero, yet scalar results of dot products can also be negative.

Subsubsection Computation of a Dot Product Using Vector Components

Coming back to our basic dot product equation, we can compute the dot products of unit vectors \(\ihat\) and \(\jhat\text{.}\) The dot product of \(\ihat\) and \(\ihat\) is:

\begin{equation*} \ihat \cdot \ihat = (1) (1)\cos = 1 \end{equation*}

As the length of \(\ihat\) is just 1, equation result is dependent on the \(\cos \theta\text{.}\) Of course, in this case \(\theta = 0\degree\) and we know that the \(\cos 0\degree = 1 \text{.}\)

Similarly, the orthogonal unit vectors \(\ihat\) and \(\jhat\) are \(90\degree\) apart so,

\begin{equation*} \ihat \cdot \jhat = (1) (1)\cos 90\degree = 0 \end{equation*}

Hence, the dot product of parallel vectors (or components) is the product of their lengths, and the dot product of perpendicular vectors is zero. Thus, we can also write that a dot product is equal to the sum-product of the like components:

\begin{equation} \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} =A_x B_x + A_y B_y = (A )( B)\cos \theta\tag{3.6} \end{equation}

Subsubsection Applications of the 2D Dot Product

Now, let's look geometrically at the amount of force \(\vec{A}\) which is in the direction force \(\vec{B}\) as shown in Figure 3.14.

Recall that our goal was to find the amount of tension force \(\vec{A}\) which is parallel to tension force \(\vec{B}\text{.}\) We see that the adjacent side of our new right triangle can be shown to be equal to \(A \cos \theta\text{.}\) This appears to be the answer to our question, but how is it this value related to the dot product? It turns out that the dot product gives us a bit more than just \(A \cos \theta\text{.}\) Rearranging the right side of the basic dot product equation we can see that

\begin{equation} \vec{A}\cdot \vec{B}=( B )( A )\cos \theta =B \left \| proj_{\vec{B}}\vec{A}\right \|\label{projection}\tag{3.7} \end{equation}

So, the dot product of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) gives us the projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) times the magnitude of \vec{B}. So, to find the scalar value of the projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) we write

\begin{equation} \left \| proj_{\vec{B}}\vec{A}\right \|=\underset{\text{calculus version}}{\left \| \frac{\left (\vec{A}\cdot \vec{B} \right )\vec{B}}{B^2}\right\|}=\frac{\vec{A}\cdot \vec{B}}{B}=\underset{\text{quick version}}{\vec{A}\cdot \vec{\hat{B}}}\\\tag{3.8} \end{equation}

While you might have learned the ‘calculus version’ if you have taken Calculus III there are simpler equivalent versions available. Rearranging (3.7) above, the projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) divided by the length of \(\vec{B}\text{.}\) This equation can be further simplified to the dot product of \(\vec{A}\) and the unit vector \(\hat{B}\text{.}\) As the unit vector \(\hat{B}\) has already been divided by the length of vector, \(\left \| \vec{B} \right \|\text{.}\)

One final modification you may need to perform is to find the vector projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\text{,}\) as opposed to the scalar value which we solve for in equation (3.7) above. To project the scalar value onto \(\vec{B}\text{,}\) multiply the scalar projection you solve for above by the unit vector \(\hat{\vec{B}}\text{.}\) In reference to our earlier cable example, the vector projection of \(\vec{A}\) onto \(\vec{B}\) would be the tension components of cable \(\vec{A}\) which are parallel to cable \(\vec{B}\text{.}\)

\begin{equation} \proj_{\vec{B}}\vec{A}=\underset{\text{calculus version}}{\frac{\left (\vec{A}\cdot \vec{B} \right )\vec{B}}{B^2}}=\underset{\text{quick version}}{\left (\vec{A}\cdot \hat{B} \right )\hat{B}}\tag{3.9} \end{equation}

Subsubsection Unit Vectors for Any Line

We talked above about the unit vectors along the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) axes, but you can also find the unit vector of any other 2D line. The rules for non-axes unit vectors are the same in that they have a length of 1 dimensionless unit.

Unit Vectors Using Sine and Cosine.

While unit vectors may seem to be a new concept to you, we will demonstrate in this section how you likely have been using unit vector components since you learned your trigonometric identities of sine and cosine. Let us use the final equivalent equation from Table 3.3 above:

\begin{equation*} \vec{F}=F \left < \cos \theta, \sin \theta \right >\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now focus on the vector components inside the angled brackets in the equation above. Draw a right triangle with the perpendicular sides horizontal and vertical and a hypotenuse of 1. Insert the angle \(\theta\) between the horizontal side and the hypotenuse. What equations do you find for the horizontal and vertical sides? You should discover that \(cos\ \theta\) and \(sin\ \theta\) are the horizontal and vertical components of the unit vector along \(\vec{F}\text{.}\)

To further prove this fact, let us look at unit vectors in the context of the Pythagorean Theorem. The Pythagorean Theorem tells us that the sum of the squares of the length of both orthogonal legs of a right triangle must equal the square of the length of the hypotenuse. So, if \(\cos\ \theta\) and \(\sin\ \theta\) are the orthogonal components of the unit vector \(\hat{F}\) and the length of the unit vector is 1, then putting these values into the Pythagorean Theorem gives us:

\begin{equation*} \left ( \cos \theta \right )^2 + \left (\sin \theta \right) ^2 = 1^2 \end{equation*}

which can be further simplified to

\begin{equation*} \cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta = 1 \end{equation*}

Reflect for a few seconds if the equation above looks familiar. It should ring a bell from your earlier learning in geometry as the equation for a circle with a radius of 1 unit. Hence, no matter what the angle \(\theta\) from a horizontal axis to a line, the terms \(\cos\ \theta\) and \(\sin\ \theta\) make up the components of the unit vector along the line. Note that if the angle \(\theta\) is measured from a vertical axis, then the sine and cosine flip, with \(\sin\ \theta\) defining the horizontal component and the \(\cos\ \theta\) defining the vertical component of the unit vector.

Unit Vectors Using Components.

Given an arbitrary vector \(\vec{F}\text{,}\) you can use the steps below to find the corresponding unit vector.

Steps to create a unit vector of vector \(\vec{F}\text{.}\)

  1. Define the vector in component form

    \begin{equation*} \vec{F} = \left \langle F_x, F_y \right \rangle \end{equation*}

  2. Compute the magnitude of the vector using the Pythagorean Theorem

    \begin{equation*} F = |\vec{F}| = \sqrt {{F_x}^2 + {F_y}^2} \end{equation*}

  3. Divide the components by the overall length

    \begin{equation*} \widehat{\vec{F}}=\frac{\vec{F}}{|\vec{F}|} = \frac{\left \langle F_x, F_y \right \rangle}{F} \end{equation*}

To again emphasize that unit vectors are pure direction, we can use the definitions of vectors and scalars to show that dividing a vector by its magnitude just leaves the direction.

\begin{equation*} \text{unit vector} = \frac{\text{vector}}{\text{magnitude}}=\frac{\cancel{\text{magnitude}} \cdot\text{direction}}{\cancel{\text{magnitude}}} = \text{direction} \end{equation*}
Checks for Unit Vectors.

Given the properties of unit vectors, there are a couple of conceptual checks you can make after computing unit vector components which can save you subsequent errors.

  1. All the signs of unit vector components need to match the signs of the original position vector. A unit vector has the same line of action and sense as the position vector but is scaled down to just one unit in magnitude.
  2. All unit vector components must not be less than -1 or greater than 1. If the total magnitude of a unit vector is 1, then it is impossible to have any components greater than a value of 1.
